## Overview

Led the development and productization of a demand forecasting system used by pharmaceutical sales teams across Europe. The system predicts short-term demand across 100,000+ products, enabling better inventory planning and more informed sales decisions.

The platform combined deep learning models with scalable data pipelines to deliver accurate forecasts that directly impacted supply chain efficiency and revenue.

## Problem

- Demand planning was largely manual or based on simple heuristics, leading to frequent overstocking and stockouts.
- Forecast accuracy was insufficient for short-term (30-day) planning at product-level granularity.
- Sales and supply chain teams lacked a unified system to translate historical data into actionable forecasts.
- Inefficient inventory allocation led to lost sales, excess holding costs, and operational inefficiencies.

## Constraints

- Large-scale forecasting across 100,000+ SKUs with varying demand patterns.
- Highly noisy and sparse time series data across regions and products.
- Need for reliable short-term forecasts (≈30-day horizon).
- System had to be productionized for non-technical sales teams.
- Cross-region variability in demand patterns and seasonality.

## Approach

Framed the problem as a multi-horizon time series forecasting task at scale.

Built a hybrid modeling approach combining:
- Deep learning models (Temporal Convolutional Networks, Temporal Fusion Transformer) for capturing temporal patterns
- Gradient boosting models for structured/tabular signals and robustness

Focused on:
- Feature engineering (temporal signals, seasonality, product and regional context)
- Model ensembling to improve generalization across product categories
- Building a production pipeline that could reliably retrain and serve forecasts

## System Design

The system follows an offline training and batch inference architecture.

Historical sales data is ingested and transformed into time series features, including lag features, seasonal indicators, and product-level metadata. Models are trained periodically and generate forecasts for a fixed horizon (e.g., 30 days).

Forecast outputs are stored and exposed through downstream tools used by sales and inventory planning teams.

## Key Decisions

### Use a hybrid modeling approach instead of a single model

Different product categories exhibited very different demand patterns. Deep learning models captured long-term temporal dependencies, while gradient boosting models handled structured features more reliably.

Combining both approaches improved robustness and overall accuracy across the full product catalog.

### Optimize for short-term forecasting accuracy

Focused specifically on 30-day forecasts rather than longer horizons.

This aligned better with operational decision-making (inventory and sales planning) and allowed models to perform more reliably with available data.

### Prioritize feature engineering over model complexity

Feature quality (lags, seasonality, product context) had a larger impact than incremental model improvements.

Investing in feature pipelines improved both deep learning and tree-based models, making the system more stable and easier to iterate on.

## Results & Impact

- **Forecast Accuracy:** ~95% accuracy for 30-day forecasts
- **Inventory Efficiency:** +25% improvement in inventory management efficiency
- **Revenue Impact:** $1M+ annual revenue contribution
- **Operational Impact:** Reduced stockouts and overstock across regions

The system enabled more precise inventory allocation, reduced waste in perishable products, and improved alignment between supply and demand.

## Tradeoffs

- Deep learning models increased training complexity and infrastructure requirements.
- Batch forecasting limited responsiveness to sudden demand shifts.
- Model performance varied across long-tail products with sparse historical data.
- Explainability was limited for deep learning components, requiring additional interpretation layers.

## Learnings

- Hybrid modeling is critical when dealing with heterogeneous demand patterns at scale.
- Short-term forecasting is often more actionable and reliable than long-horizon predictions.
- Feature engineering drives more impact than model selection in many real-world systems.
- Productionization (pipelines, retraining, monitoring) is as important as model performance.

## Future Work

- Introduce online updates for faster adaptation to demand shifts.
- Improve long-tail product forecasting with transfer learning or hierarchical models.
- Add explainability layers to improve trust with business users.
- Explore probabilistic forecasting to better capture uncertainty.
